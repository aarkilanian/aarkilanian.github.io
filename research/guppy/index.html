Visual Ecology

Animals display an astounding range of colouration and colour patterns. This colouration can serve animals in a number of important ways, most notably in diverse modes of communication both within and between species. Some colour patterns are bright and contrast strongly with their background to create highly conspicuous colouration. This form of intense colouration can serve between species as a warning signal to potential predators that an organism is unpalatable, a strategy known as aposematic colouration, or, alternatively, an organism can mimic other unpalatable species to reap the same benefits, a strategy known as Batesian mimicry. These conspicuous colour patterns can also be used by conspecifics as proxies for fitness in many species. Other times, colour patterns can be designed to be inconspicuous serving an entirely different function. Inconspicuous colour patterns can be used to avoid detection by predators in various ways. For example, this can be in the form of disruptive colouration which breaks up the outline of an animal against its background or camouflage through background matching which involves the expression of colour patterns similar to that of the background.

For years scientists have been interested in the causes and consequences of these colour polymorphisms, but recently the geometry of these patterns has become a topic of more intense research. Traditional quantification of animal colouration in a famous ecological model system, guppies in the Trinidadian Paria and Marianne stream system, has traditionally used a method of colour patch identification involving the manual outlining of patches on guppy photographs and the subsequent classification of these patches into broad colour categories: black, orange, fuzzy black, yellow, silver, blue, green, purple. In light of new advances in pattern recognition and quantification I implemented a novel method, adjacency analysis, to the colour patterns of Trinidadian guppies.

Adjacency analysis proceeds by laying a grid of transects over a colour pattern and outputting different metrics related to adjacent colours. This gives information on patch sizes and the orientation of colour patches. I conlcuded that this crude computational approach does not provide richer information than traditional methods. However, many new techniques have been developped and are still being developped to automate colour quantification in ecological studies.
