Spatial Stream Ecology

Rivers and streams are among the most threatened ecosystems today faced with increased land use modification and a changing climate. These systems offer some specific and essential ecosystem services and hold unique biodiversity. In the rapidly urbanizing and growing region of the St. Lawrence Lowlands these ecosystems are threatened by fragmentation at the hands of increasing infrastructure to support a growing developing human population; namely the construction of roads and hydroelectric dams to meet the growing energy requirements of the population threaten to fragment the river networks of this region.

Maintaining connectivity in these systems allows fish to migrate and complete their lifecycle, and in the longer term shift their ranges in response to a changing climate. Ultimately prioritizing connectivity will lead to more diverse and resilient fish communities in response to increasing threats such as climate and land use change. For this reason I am doing work to quantify the connectivity of the major stream systems in the St. Lawrence Lowlands for fish. This project is in partnership with the MFFP (Quebec ministry for forest, fauna, and parks), MELCC (Quebec ministry for the environment and the fight against climate change), and the MTQ (Quebec ministy of transport).
